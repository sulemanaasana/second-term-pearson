The Coat of Arms

An Emblem of Sovereignty, Justice & Heritage

Designer of Ghana’s Coat of Arms, representing authority, unity, and national values. — Adopted 4 March 1964

Historical Background

Origin & Adoption

Ghana's Coat of Arms was officially adopted on 4 March 1964, replacing earlier colonial heraldic designs. It was crafted to reflect the distinct identity, history, and aspirations of a newly independent nation — moving decisively away from symbols rooted in British imperial imagery. Every element was chosen deliberately to tell the story of Ghana's people, their resources, their governance, and their values.

National Motto

"Freedom and Justice" — A Daily Civic Standard

The motto inscribed at the base of the Coat of Arms — "Freedom and Justice" — is the most concentrated civic responsibility statement in all of Ghana's national symbols. These three words are not a description of Ghana's past. They are a daily standard against which every citizen, every institution, and every government must measure their conduct.

"Freedom and Justice" — these words encapsulate the founding principles of the Ghanaian state: the freedom won through independence, and the justice that must govern every citizen's life under the law.

Freedom means defending the constitutional rights of every Ghanaian — your own and those of others. Justice means treating every person fairly regardless of tribe, religion, gender, or economic status. Every interaction a citizen has with another person is an opportunity to either live or betray this motto.

Elements & Their Meaning

What Each Element Represents

🦅 The Golden Eagle (Displayed at the Top)

The eagle is a symbol of strength, vision, and authority. It represents Ghana's aspirations to soar above adversity and stand as a powerful, respected nation among the community of nations. Civically, it calls every citizen to have the vision and courage to see beyond immediate personal interest to the long-term good of the nation.

🛡️ The Divided Shield

The shield is divided into four quarters by a green St George's Cross, with a black five-pointed star at the centre of the cross. The cross recalls the role of Christianity in Ghana's history while the star echoes the flag's Pan-African symbol — placing the Black Star at the heart of the nation's official emblem.

First Quarter (Top Left) — Red Background with Black Star

Represents the Black Star of African freedom, echoing the national flag and affirming Ghana's sovereignty and leadership role on the continent.

Second Quarter (Top Right) — Gold Background with Sword & Ceremonial Staff

The crossed sword and staff represent the traditional power of Ghanaian chieftaincy — honouring the deep roots of governance and authority in Ghanaian culture and the role of traditional leadership in national life.

Third Quarter (Bottom Left) — Blue Background with Cocoa Tree

The cocoa tree is Ghana's most historically significant agricultural symbol. It represents the agricultural wealth that powered Ghana's early economy and the diligence of Ghanaian farmers who built national prosperity from the soil. Civically, it calls every citizen to work hard and contribute to national development.

Fourth Quarter (Bottom Right) — Gold Background with Castle

Represents Osu Castle (now Jubilee House), the seat of Ghana's government. It symbolises the authority of the democratic state and the continuity of governance from colonial administration to sovereign self-rule.

Two Eagles as Supporters

Flanking the shield on either side, these eagles reinforce the themes of strength, protection, and national vigilance — the duty of every citizen to guard Ghana's freedom and institutions.

Civic Relevance

Real-Life Use & Patriotic Significance

The Coat of Arms appears on all official government documents, state correspondence, Ghana's currency, public seals, passports, and national institutions. It is the official mark of governmental authority and is treated with the highest respect in state protocol. Citizens encounter it on their passports and identity documents — a constant reminder of their national identity and rights.

Every element of the Coat of Arms carries a civic instruction: the eagle demands vision and courage; the cocoa tree demands diligent labour; the castle demands respect for and accountability from government; the motto demands Freedom and Justice in every daily interaction. The Coat of Arms is Ghana's civic education compressed into a single emblem.
